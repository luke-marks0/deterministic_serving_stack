Timing covert-channel capacity visualizer

Drag the knobs to see how attacker-side modulation, network jitter, and bucket-pacing defenses change the bps an exfiltrator can extract from a token-streaming endpoint.

Why model this

A streaming LLM endpoint emits tokens with measurable inter-arrival times. An attacker who controls (or biases) generation can encode bits into those gaps; an outside observer who sees the stream timestamps can decode them. This is a classic timing covert channel, and our experiments in this directory measure its raw capacity (~150 bps undefended at 16-PAM, 50 packets/s) and how a bucket pacer collapses it.

The point of this page is to make the knobs explorable. Two questions matter for the IEEE S&P paper:

  • Given a defense (bucket pacer at B ms), what's the residual capacity an attacker can still achieve, and which knob trades against which?
  • Where does the “200 bps” number from our prior experiments come from, and is it tight or generous? (Spoiler: generous.)

The carrier is the packet, not the token

Subtle but load-bearing. The timing observable is one timestamp per packet. A serving stack that flushes one token per SSE event gives the attacker one symbol per token; a stack that batches k tokens per flush gives the attacker one symbol per k tokens. So bps = packet_rate · bits_per_packet, and tokens_per_packet only changes the per-token framing.

Model

The attacker uses M-PAM on inter-packet delays:

  • Symbol s ∈ {0..M-1} is encoded as a target inter-packet delay d_s = base + s · step (ms).
  • A bucket pacer (if active) holds each packet to the next bucket boundary: emit at ⌈t/B⌉·B.
  • Network jitter adds Gaussian noise σ on top.
  • The receiver decodes by nearest-neighbor on observed inter-arrival.

Bucket pacing has two effects: (a) it aliases symbols whose target delays fall in the same bucket — pure information loss — and (b) it raises the effective separation between distinguishable levels.

σ is delay-domain noise

The receiver decodes inter-arrival delays. σ here is the std of jitter on the inter-arrival, not on individual timestamps. (If your model has per-timestamp jitter σ_t, the delay-domain σ is roughly σ_t·√2.) Default σ=0.55ms reproduces our undefended SER=0.0625 measurement.

Closed form (optimal-decoder upper bound)

The bucket channel is two-mode discrete + Gaussian noise. When the sender intends to emit at offset Δ_s = base + step·s from the previous packet, and the previous packet was released at a bucket boundary at random phase relative to the bucket grid, the observed inter-arrival is

Y = q_s·B  with prob 1 − p_s
Y = (q_s+1)·B  with prob p_s

where q_s = ⌊Δ_s/B⌋ and p_s = (Δ_s mod B)/B. Then Gaussian jitter σ is added on the wire. Capacity is the mutual information of this channel:

bits_per_packet = (1/M)·Σ_s E_Y|s[log₂(p(Y|s) / p(Y))]
bps = effective_rate · bits_per_packet

The expectation is taken over the 2-component Gaussian mixture Y|s, computed via 16-point Gauss-Hermite quadrature around each peak. When B = 0 the formula reduces to plain M-PAM AWGN.

Why the previous models were wrong. Two earlier attempts in this project's history: (1) the BSC approximation log₂(M)·(1−H_b(SER)) is a loose lower bound that gets beaten by MC under moderate noise; (2) the M_eff = ⌊range/B⌋+1 discretization is a hard cliff that says capacity = 0 when bucket exceeds modulation range, but the channel never actually closes — even one bit per packet from "did this packet fall in the same bucket as the previous one or the next" survives. Both were caught by the validation suite.

Monte-Carlo (naive-decoder estimate)

The MC button simulates a naive receiver — one that doesn't know about pacing and decodes nearest-neighbor against the original (base, step) M-PAM grid, like the harness in bucket_experiment.py. So MC ≤ closed form: the gap between them is the value an attacker would gain from explicit channel modeling.

Validation against experiments

Calibrating σ against the undefended case (16-PAM, base=5ms, step=2ms, 50 pps): closed form predicts SER=0.065, MC gives 0.065, observed is 0.062 — clean agreement, all three within MC noise. Under bucket pacing the three diverge: at B=10ms, closed form says 0 (optimal decoder reads the bucket grid), MC says 0.77 (naive decoder is fully aliased), the harness sits at 0.34. The harness number is between because its pacer is a leaky-bucket release (drain queue every B ms) rather than a hard arrival-time quantizer — packets arriving in the same drain interval go out back-to-back instead of all snapping to the next boundary. The visualizer models the hard-quantizer abstraction; treat the harness data as one realization of a softer pacing scheme.
